Foundations of Computing & Quantum Concepts

Before talking about qubits, it helps to be precise about what a classical computer actually does — and where it runs out of road. This unit sets that baseline, then opens the door to why quantum computing exists at all.

01 From Switches to Bits

Every classical computer, no matter how advanced, is built from bits — a unit of information that can be in exactly one of two states, 0 or 1. Physically, a bit is usually a tiny switch (a transistor) that is either "off" or "on".

Click the switch below — notice it can only ever be in one state at a time. That single constraint is the entire foundation of classical computing.

02 Logic Gates & Binary Operations

Bits become useful once you combine them with logic gates — small circuits that take one or two bits in and produce a bit out, according to a fixed rule. Toggle the inputs below and watch the output update live.

03 Composing Gates: The Half-Adder

A single gate is limited, but chaining gates together builds arbitrarily complex logic. A half-adder adds two one-bit numbers using exactly the two gates you just played with: XOR computes the sum bit, AND computes the carry bit. This same idea — small fixed components, wired together — is exactly how billions of transistors become a CPU, and exactly how a handful of quantum gates become a quantum circuit in Unit III.

SUM (A⊕B) = 0    CARRY (A·B) = 0

Try A=1, B=1 — sum rolls over to 0 and carry becomes 1, exactly like 1+1=10 in binary.

04 Moore's Law — and Its Wall

For fifty years, engineers doubled the number of transistors on a chip roughly every two years (Moore's Law), making classical computers exponentially faster. That trend is now hitting physical limits.

As transistors shrink toward a few nanometres, they run into real physical effects — heat that can't be dissipated fast enough, current that leaks across "off" switches, and electrons that quantum tunnel straight through barriers that should stop them. Ironically, the same quantum effects that break classical transistors are exactly what a quantum computer is designed to exploit.

05 Enter Quantum Computing

A quantum computer is not a faster version of your laptop. It's a fundamentally different kind of machine that stores and processes information using superposition and entanglement — phenomena with no classical counterpart. For most everyday tasks (browsing, spreadsheets, video) it offers no advantage at all.

Where it shines is a narrow but important set of problems — simulating molecules, breaking certain codes, searching unstructured data, optimisation — where the quantum approach scales fundamentally better than any classical algorithm we know. Think of it as a specialised co-processor, not a replacement.

Fundamentals of Quantum Mechanics for Computing

This is where the qubit is born. We'll build up the Bloch sphere — the single picture you'll use for the rest of the course to reason about one qubit at a time.

01 The Qubit

A classical bit is 0 or 1. A qubit is described by a state

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩

where α and β are complex numbers called amplitudes, satisfying |α|² + |β|² = 1. Squaring an amplitude gives the probability of measuring that outcome — |α|² is the chance of getting 0, |β|² the chance of getting 1.

02 Superposition, Properly Understood

A common but misleading metaphor is "a coin spinning in the air, both heads and tails at once." A qubit in superposition isn't secretly flickering between 0 and 1 — it has one single, definite, well-defined state that simply isn't 0 or 1. It only becomes 0 or 1 the moment you measure it. Before that, the amplitudes α and β are both real, both present, and both used by the computation.

05 Wave–Particle Duality & Interference

Quantum objects behave like waves that can overlap and interfere — constructively (amplifying) or destructively (cancelling) — even though they're detected as discrete particles. This is the same interference that quantum algorithms exploit to amplify correct answers and cancel out wrong ones, as you'll see in Grover's algorithm later.

06 Multi-Qubit Systems & the No-Cloning Theorem

A single qubit needs 2 amplitudes to describe it. Put n qubits together and the joint state needs 2n amplitudes — one for every possible bit string of length n. Three qubits → 8 amplitudes; ten qubits → 1024; fifty qubits → more numbers than there are atoms in a grain of sand.

|ψ⟩ = Σ ci |i⟩    (i = 0 … 2n−1)

This is exactly what makes simulating quantum systems on a classical computer so hard — and it's the resource a quantum computer manipulates directly. Drag the slider to see how fast it grows.

21 = 2 amplitudes

The No-Cloning Theorem

You cannot build a machine that copies an arbitrary unknown quantum state perfectly — doing so would violate the linearity of quantum mechanics. This isn't an engineering limitation; it's a theorem.

It's also why quantum cryptography (Unit V) works at all: an eavesdropper can't just "photocopy" a qubit in transit and measure the copy later without disturbing the original.

Tensor Products, Concretely

|0⟩⊗|0⟩ = |00⟩  |0⟩⊗|1⟩ = |01⟩
|1⟩⊗|0⟩ = |10⟩  |1⟩⊗|1⟩ = |11⟩

Two qubits in superposition combine into a 4-dimensional space, not just 2+2 — this is where quantum computing's "exponential workspace" actually comes from.

Why an unknown state cannot be copied

Suppose a perfect copying operation U could copy both basis states: U|0⟩|0⟩=|0⟩|0⟩ and U|1⟩|0⟩=|1⟩|1⟩. Quantum operations are linear, so applying the same operation to |+⟩=(|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 must give:

U|+⟩|0⟩ = (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2

But two genuine copies would be |+⟩|+⟩=(|00⟩+|01⟩+|10⟩+|11⟩)/2. The results differ. CNOT copies a basis value but entangles a superposition. The restriction applies to an arbitrary unknown state; orthogonal states can still be distinguished and copied.

no_cloning_demo.py
import numpy as np
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector, state_fidelity

attempt = QuantumCircuit(2)
attempt.h(0)       # the state to be copied is |+>
attempt.cx(0, 1)   # CNOT is the natural copying attempt
actual = Statevector.from_instruction(attempt)

plus = Statevector([1, 1]) / np.sqrt(2)
two_copies = plus.tensor(plus)

print("CNOT result:", actual.probabilities_dict())
print("Two copies:", two_copies.probabilities_dict())
print("Fidelity:", state_fidelity(actual, two_copies))  # 0.5, not 1

02 Phase Gates — S and T

S and T don't flip the qubit at all — they rotate its phase around the z-axis of the Bloch sphere. You can't see their effect on |0⟩ or |1⟩ themselves, but on an equatorial state (after a Hadamard) the rotation is plain to see. These two cards start at |+⟩ so you can watch it happen.

Global phase and relative phase

Multiplying an entire state by eiγ is a global phase and changes no measurement probability. A phase difference between basis components is relative phase and can change interference.

|+⟩=(|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2    |−⟩=(|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2

Both states give 50:50 results in the Z basis, but H|+⟩=|0⟩ while H|−⟩=|1⟩. The second Hadamard converts their hidden relative-phase difference into a directly measurable difference.

S

S Gate (Phase)

[[1,0],[0,i]]

Rotates the phase by 90° (π/2) around the z-axis. Applying it twice equals a Z gate.

T

T Gate (π/8)

[[1,0],[0,eiπ/4]]

Rotates the phase by 45° (π/4) around the z-axis. Applying it twice equals an S gate.

from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector

qc = QuantumCircuit(1)
qc.h(0)     # move to the equator: |+> = (|0>+|1>)/sqrt(2)

state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
print(state)                  # probabilities are unchanged - only the relative phase shifted
print(state.probabilities())  # still [0.5, 0.5]

03 CNOT — A Two-Qubit Gate

CNOT (controlled-X) acts on two qubits: a control and a target. If the control is |1⟩, the target is flipped; if the control is |0⟩, nothing happens. Toggle the control qubit below.

Prerequisite theory, in simple language

A single qubit can be in |0⟩, |1⟩, or a superposition of both. With two qubits there are four basis states: |00⟩, |01⟩, |10⟩ and |11⟩. In this notation the right-hand bit is q0 and the left-hand bit is q1, matching Qiskit’s displayed bit order.

Hadamard gate (H)

H changes a definite |0⟩ into an equal superposition. It does not randomly choose a value at this stage; it creates two probability amplitudes that can later interfere.

H|0⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2

Controlled-NOT gate (CNOT)

CNOT reads neither qubit. It applies X to the target only on the branch where the control is |1⟩.

|00⟩→|00⟩   |01⟩→|11⟩
|10⟩→|10⟩   |11⟩→|01⟩

Mathematical explanation: creating a Bell state

Begin with both qubits in |0⟩. The joint state is formed using the tensor product:

|0⟩⊗|0⟩ = |00⟩

Apply H only to q0. Because q1 remains |0⟩, the two-qubit state becomes:

(I⊗H)|00⟩ = (|00⟩ + |01⟩)/√2

Now apply CNOT with q0 as control and q1 as target. The |00⟩ branch remains |00⟩. In the |01⟩ branch, q0 is 1, so q1 flips and |01⟩ becomes |11⟩:

CNOT (|00⟩ + |01⟩)/√2 = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2 = |Φ⁺⟩

This final state cannot be separated into an independent state for q0 and another independent state for q1. The qubits are entangled. Measurement gives 00 or 11 with probability 1/2 each; 01 and 10 have zero probability in an ideal circuit.

Circuit and gate-by-gate evolution

  1. Initialise: QuantumCircuit(2) starts in |00⟩.
  2. Superpose: h(0) creates equal |00⟩ and |01⟩ amplitudes.
  3. Entangle: cx(0,1) correlates the target with the control, producing |Φ⁺⟩.
  4. Inspect: calculate the statevector before adding measurement; measurement destroys the statevector’s coherent phase information.
  5. Measure: repeated shots reveal the predicted 00/11 correlation.

CNOT is exactly how entanglement gets created on real hardware: apply a Hadamard to the control, then CNOT onto the target, and the two qubits become a Bell pair — the |00⟩ and |11⟩ outcomes become equally likely, and nothing else.

bell_state.py
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit

04 The Toffoli Gate (CCX) — A Universal Building Block

The Toffoli gate has two controls and one target: the target flips only if both controls are |1⟩ — it's a quantum AND gate wired into a reversible circuit. Together with single-qubit gates, Toffoli is enough to build any classical logic circuit reversibly, and is a core ingredient of the arithmetic inside Shor's algorithm. Toggle the two controls below.

toffoli.py

from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector

qc = QuantumCircuit(3)
qc.x(0); qc.x(1)  # set both controls to |1>
qc.ccx(0, 1, 2)         # target (qubit 2) flips only if both controls are 1

Probability, basis and collapse

For |ψ⟩=α|0⟩+β|1⟩, a measurement in the computational or Z basis returns 0 with probability |α|² and 1 with probability |β|². The normalization rule |α|²+|β|²=1 guarantees that the probabilities total one.

|+⟩=(|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2  ⇒  P(0)=P(1)=1/2

One shot returns one classical outcome, never “half 0 and half 1.” Many shots require preparing the same state again for every run. They estimate a distribution across repeated preparations; they do not expose the amplitudes of one individual qubit.

Measurement also depends on the chosen basis. H|+⟩=|0⟩, so placing H before a Z-basis measurement is equivalent to measuring the original state in the X basis.

measurement_basis.py
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator

def measure_plus(basis, shots=1000):
    qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1)
    qc.h(0)              # prepare |+>
    if basis == "X":
        qc.h(0)          # rotate X basis into the measurable Z basis
    qc.measure(0, 0)
    return AerSimulator().run(qc, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

print("Z-basis:", measure_plus("Z"))  # about 50% 0 and 50% 1
print("X-basis:", measure_plus("X"))  # always 0 because |+> is an X eigenstate

Quantum Computation

Zooming out from individual gates: what counting as a "model" of quantum computation, what makes a problem worth handing to a quantum computer, and what it actually takes to build one.

01 Models for Computation

Circuit Model

The model used throughout this course — qubits flow through a sequence of gates, then get measured. The most common and best-supported model on real hardware today.

Adiabatic Model

The system is slowly evolved from an easy-to-prepare state into one whose lowest-energy configuration encodes the answer to a problem. Used by quantum annealers.

Measurement-Based

Computation proceeds purely by measuring qubits in a large pre-entangled cluster state, one at a time, in a chosen order.

02 Which Problems Benefit?

Classical complexity theory sorts problems into classes like P (easy to solve) and NP (easy to check). Quantum computers add a new class, BQP — problems efficiently solvable on a quantum computer. BQP is believed to contain some problems outside efficient classical reach (like factoring large numbers), but it does not make every hard problem easy. Most NP-hard problems are not known to have quantum speedups.

03 Conditions for Quantum Computation

  • Well-characterized qubits that can be scaled up to many copies.
  • Reliable initialisation — the ability to reset every qubit to a known starting state.
  • Long coherence times — the qubit must hold its state much longer than a gate takes to run.
  • A universal gate set — enough gate types to build any quantum algorithm.
  • High-fidelity measurement — reading out each qubit's final state reliably.

04 Physical Realisations

Superconducting Circuits

Tiny circuits cooled near absolute zero; qubits are quantised current/voltage states. Used by IBM, Google.

Trapped Ions

Individual charged atoms held by electromagnetic fields; qubits are electronic energy levels, controlled with lasers.

Harmonic Oscillator QC

Uses the evenly-spaced energy "ladder" of a quantum harmonic oscillator (e.g. an ion's motion, or a microwave resonator) as computational levels.

Optical / Photonic

Single photons act as qubits, steered with beam splitters and mirrors; travels easily through fibre, but two-photon gates are hard.

05 Decoherence, Error Correction & the NISQ Era

Real qubits leak information into their environment — decoherence — and gates are never perfectly precise. Today's machines are firmly in the NISQ era (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum): tens to a few thousand qubits, with no full error correction yet. The five conditions in block 03 are sometimes called DiVincenzo's criteria, after the physicist who first listed them as requirements for a practical quantum computer.

The fix is quantum error correction: spread one "logical" qubit's information across several noisy "physical" qubits so that an error on any single one can be detected and reversed — without ever directly measuring (and collapsing) the protected information.

Encoding, syndrome and correction

The logical state α|0⟩+β|1⟩ is encoded as α|000⟩+β|111⟩. The amplitudes are not measured. Two parity checks only reveal whether neighbouring physical qubits agree, producing a syndrome that identifies one flipped qubit.

00: no error   10: q0 flipped   11: q1 flipped   01: q2 flipped

Applying X to the indicated qubit restores the encoded state. This code handles one X error, not phase errors or two simultaneous bit flips.

bit_flip_code.py
import numpy as np
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector, partial_trace, state_fidelity

theta = 0.9
qc = QuantumCircuit(5)      # q0-q2 data; q3-q4 syndrome ancillas
qc.ry(theta, 0)       # prepare an arbitrary logical state on q0
qc.cx(0, 1); qc.cx(0, 2)
qc.x(1)               # inject one X error on physical qubit q1

qc.cx(0, 3); qc.cx(1, 3)  # syndrome s01
qc.cx(1, 4); qc.cx(2, 4)  # syndrome s12

qc.x(4); qc.ccx(3, 4, 0); qc.x(4)
qc.ccx(3, 4, 1)
qc.x(3); qc.ccx(3, 4, 2); qc.x(3)

qc.cx(1, 4); qc.cx(2, 4)  # clear syndrome ancillas
qc.cx(0, 3); qc.cx(1, 3)
qc.cx(0, 2); qc.cx(0, 1)  # decode

recovered = partial_trace(Statevector.from_instruction(qc), [1,2,3,4])
target = Statevector([np.cos(theta/2), np.sin(theta/2)])
print(state_fidelity(recovered, target))  # 1.0: the logical state is recovered

02 Optimisation & Machine Learning

Optimisation

Algorithms like QAOA encode a problem (e.g. routing, scheduling) as an energy landscape, then use quantum circuits to search for low-energy — i.e. good — solutions faster than classical heuristics in some cases.

Machine Learning

Quantum feature maps embed classical data into a high-dimensional quantum state space, where patterns that are hard to separate classically may become easier to classify.

How variational quantum algorithms work

QAOA and VQE are hybrid algorithms. A parameterised circuit prepares a trial state; a classical optimiser changes its angles using an objective estimated from the quantum state.

QAOA: alternate a cost operator with a mixer to favour better bit strings.

|ψ(γ,β)⟩=UB(β)UC(γ)|+⟩⊗n

VQE: minimise the expected Hamiltonian energy.

E(θ)=⟨ψ(θ)|H|ψ(θ)⟩ ≥ E0

These small examples illustrate the workflow; they do not by themselves demonstrate an advantage over classical optimisation.

qaoa_maxcut_sketch.py
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit

# Conceptual sketch of one QAOA layer for a MaxCut-style cost function
def qaoa_layer(qc, edges, gamma, beta):
    for (i, j) in edges:
        qc.cx(i, j)
        qc.rz(2*gamma, j)   # cost layer: encodes the graph's edges
        qc.cx(i, j)
    for q in range(qc.num_qubits):
        qc.rx(2*beta, q)     # mixer layer: spreads amplitude to explore solutions

qc = QuantumCircuit(4)
qc.h(range(4))
qaoa_layer(qc, edges=[(0,1),(1,2),(2,3),(3,0)], gamma=0.8, beta=0.4)
qc.measure_all()
# a classical optimiser tunes gamma, beta to maximise the cut value, layer by layer
vqe_ground_state.py
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector, SparsePauliOp

H = SparsePauliOp.from_list([("Z", 0.5), ("X", -1.0)])

def ansatz(theta):
    qc = QuantumCircuit(1)
    qc.ry(theta[0], 0); qc.rz(theta[1], 0)
    return qc

def energy(theta):
    psi = Statevector.from_instruction(ansatz(theta))
    return float(np.real(psi.expectation_value(H)))

result = minimize(energy, [0.2, 0.1], method="COBYLA")
exact = np.linalg.eigvalsh(H.to_matrix())[0]
print("VQE energy:", result.fun)
print("Exact energy:", exact)  # the values should closely agree

03 Grover's Algorithm — Searching with Interference

Classically, finding one marked item among N requires checking, on average, N/2 items. Grover's algorithm finds it in roughly √N steps by using interference: an oracle flips the sign of the marked item's amplitude, then a diffusion step reflects every amplitude about the average — which boosts the marked item and suppresses the rest.

Amplitude amplification for four possibilities

Two Hadamards assign amplitude 1/2 to each of 00, 01, 10 and 11. The oracle marks the answer by changing only its sign, from +1/2 to −1/2; probabilities are still 1/4 at this point because a sign change alone is not visible to measurement.

Before oracle: [1/2, 1/2, 1/2, 1/2]
After marking |10⟩: [1/2, 1/2, −1/2, 1/2]

The new mean amplitude is 1/4. Diffusion maps every amplitude a to 2×mean−a. The unmarked amplitudes become 0 and the marked amplitude becomes 1. This is interference—not parallel checking followed by simply reading every answer.

Number of iterations ≈ floor(π√N/4)

4-item search space {00, 01, 10, 11}, marked item = 10. For N=4, exactly one Grover iteration reaches 100% certainty.

from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator
from qiskit.visualization import plot_histogram

# 2 qubits, search space {00,01,10,11}, marked state |10>
qc = QuantumCircuit(2)

qc.h([0, 1])        # step 1: equal superposition

# step 2: oracle - flip the phase of |10>
qc.x(1)
qc.cz(0, 1)
qc.x(1)

# step 3: diffusion operator (inversion about the mean)
qc.h([0, 1])
qc.x([0, 1])
qc.cz(0, 1)
qc.x([0, 1])
qc.h([0, 1])

sim = AerSimulator()
counts = sim.run(qc, shots=1000).result().get_counts()
plot_histogram(counts)   # ~100% '10'

04 Shor's Algorithm — Factoring via Periods (Conceptual)

Shor's algorithm factors large numbers exponentially faster than the best known classical method — the reason quantum computers threaten RSA encryption. The core trick: factoring N can be reduced to finding the period r of the sequence aˣ mod N, and a quantum computer can find that period efficiently using the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT).

from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.circuit.library import QFT

# Conceptual sketch of the quantum part of Shor's algorithm:
# 1. Prepare a superposition of all possible exponents x
# 2. Compute a^x mod N into an output register (modular exponentiation)
# 3. Apply the Quantum Fourier Transform to the exponent register
# 4. Measure - results peak at multiples of (2^n_count) / period

n_count = 4  # qubits used to hold the exponent x
qc = QuantumCircuit(n_count)

qc.h(range(n_count))                       # step 1: superposition over x
# ... modular exponentiation circuit goes here ...
qc.append(QFT(n_count), range(n_count))   # step 3: extract the period
qc.measure_all()

05 Quantum Teleportation — Moving a State, Not Matter

Teleportation doesn't transmit matter or energy — it transfers an unknown qubit's exact state from Alice to Bob using one shared entangled pair plus two classical bits sent over an ordinary channel. The no-cloning theorem (Unit II) is exactly why this is needed instead of just copying: Alice's original qubit is destroyed by her measurement in the process.

Why the two classical bits are enough

After the Bell-basis operations, the three-qubit state can be regrouped by Alice’s two possible measurement bits:

1/2 [ |00⟩|ψ⟩ + |01⟩X|ψ⟩ + |10⟩Z|ψ⟩ + |11⟩XZ|ψ⟩ ]

Alice’s result does not reveal α or β. It tells Bob which known Pauli correction is needed: 00→I, 01→X, 10→Z, 11→XZ. After the correction, Bob holds |ψ⟩. Entanglement supplies the quantum correlation; the classical bits complete the protocol and enforce ordinary causal communication.

  • Alice and Bob start by sharing one Bell pair (one qubit each).
  • Alice entangles her unknown qubit |ψ⟩ with her half of the pair, then measures both — destroying the original.
  • She sends her 2-bit measurement result to Bob over a classical channel — this step cannot happen faster than light, so no information actually travels instantaneously.
  • Bob applies an X and/or Z gate to his qubit based on those 2 bits, and his qubit becomes exactly |ψ⟩.
teleportation.py
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector, partial_trace, state_fidelity

theta, phi = 1.1, 0.7
qc = QuantumCircuit(3)
qc.ry(theta, 0); qc.rz(phi, 0)  # unknown |psi> on q0

reference = QuantumCircuit(1)
reference.ry(theta, 0); reference.rz(phi, 0)
psi = Statevector.from_instruction(reference)

qc.h(1); qc.cx(1, 2)       # shared Bell pair
qc.cx(0, 1); qc.h(0)        # Bell-basis transformation
qc.cx(1, 2); qc.cz(0, 2)       # deferred X and Z corrections

final_state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
bob = partial_trace(final_state, [0, 1])
print("Teleportation fidelity:", state_fidelity(bob, psi))  # 1.0

# Deferred measurement keeps the circuit unitary for verification.
# On hardware, q0 and q1 are measured and their two bits control Bob's corrections.

From Circuit Recipes to Quantum Understanding

Programming is only the laboratory instrument. The theory below explains what a state is, why gates have their matrices, what measurement predicts, and where quantum advantage—and its limits—come from.

01 The Four Postulates and the Meaning of a Qubit

1 · State space

An isolated system is represented by a unit vector (more precisely, a ray) \(|\psi\rangle\) in a complex Hilbert space. For a qubit, \(\mathcal H=\mathbb C^2\) and \(|\psi\rangle=\alpha|0\rangle+\beta|1\rangle\), with \(|\alpha|^2+|\beta|^2=1\).

2 · Evolution

A closed system evolves by a unitary operator: \(|\psi'\rangle=U|\psi\rangle\). Unitarity, \(U^\dagger U=I\), preserves inner products and therefore total probability.

3 · Measurement

A measurement is described by operators \(\{M_m\}\) satisfying \(\sum_m M_m^\dagger M_m=I\). Outcome \(m\) occurs with probability \(p(m)=\langle\psi|M_m^\dagger M_m|\psi\rangle\) and leaves the conditional state \(M_m|\psi\rangle/\sqrt{p(m)}\).

4 · Composition

The state space of systems A and B is ℋₐ ⊗ ℋᵦ. This tensor-product rule creates joint states that cannot always be reduced to separate states for A and B.

State vector ≠ physical object

A ket is a mathematical description used to predict statistics. A physical qubit is the two-level carrier—such as two energy levels of an ion or superconducting circuit. Global phase is not observable: |ψ⟩ and eiγ|ψ⟩ represent the same physical pure state.

03 Tensor Products and Genuine Entanglement

Building a Bell state by hand\[|00\rangle\xrightarrow{H\otimes I}\frac{|00\rangle+|10\rangle}{\sqrt2}\xrightarrow{\mathrm{CNOT}}\frac{|00\rangle+|11\rangle}{\sqrt2}=|\Phi^+\rangle\]

Suppose |Φ⁺⟩ could be factored as (a|0⟩+b|1⟩)⊗(c|0⟩+d|1⟩). Its coefficients would require ac=1/√2, ad=0, bc=0, and bd=1/√2. The first and last equations require a,b,c,d all nonzero, contradicting ad=bc=0. Therefore the state is non-separable.

Correlation is not enough

A classical mixture, ½|00⟩⟨00| + ½|11⟩⟨11|, gives matching Z-basis outcomes but has no coherent off-diagonal terms. The Bell state does; it also shows basis-dependent correlations that cannot be explained by that mixture.

Schmidt decomposition · advanced

Any bipartite pure state can be written Σᵢ√λᵢ |uᵢ⟩|vᵢ⟩. Schmidt rank 1 means separable; rank greater than 1 means entangled. |Φ⁺⟩ has coefficients √½, √½ and rank 2.

04 Measurement, Born Rule, and Basis Dependence

For computational-basis projectors P₀=|0⟩⟨0| and P₁=|1⟩⟨1|, a state α|0⟩+β|1⟩ yields p(0)=|α|² and p(1)=|β|². Measurement does not uncover a hidden stored bit; it produces an outcome with probabilities fixed by the state and chosen observable.

Same state, different basis\[|+\rangle=\frac{|0\rangle+|1\rangle}{\sqrt2}\]Z measurement: \(p(0)=p(1)=\tfrac12\)X measurement with \(P_+=|+\rangle\langle+|\): \(p(+)=|\langle+|+\rangle|^2=1\)Operationally, X-basis measurement means applying \(H\), then measuring in Z.
Common misconception

“Collapse” is the conditional state update after learning an outcome. It must not replace the Born-rule calculation, and measurement basis must always be stated.

05 Why Grover Gives a √N Advantage

The oracle reflects the state vector across the subspace orthogonal to the marked answer; the diffuser reflects it about the uniform superposition |s⟩. Two reflections make a rotation in the two-dimensional plane spanned by the target |w⟩ and the uniform non-target state |r⟩.

\[|s\rangle=\sin\theta|w\rangle+\cos\theta|r\rangle,\qquad \sin\theta=\frac1{\sqrt N}\]After \(k\) Grover iterations, the success amplitude is \(\sin((2k+1)\theta)\).\[(2k+1)\theta\approx\frac\pi2\quad\Rightarrow\quad k\approx\frac\pi4\sqrt N\]Thus query complexity is \(O(\sqrt N)\), versus \(O(N)\) classically—not an exponential speedup.

07 Hardware Reality and Error Correction

Noise and decoherence

T₁ describes energy relaxation; T₂ describes loss of phase coherence. Gates must finish well within these times while control errors remain small.

Imperfect operations

Gate fidelity and readout fidelity are below 100%. Limited coupling maps force SWAP gates, increasing circuit depth and accumulated error.

NISQ constraint

Present devices have useful control but insufficient physical-qubit quality and scale for large fault-tolerant algorithms. Simulator results are upper bounds, not hardware promises.

3-qubit bit-flip|0⟩→|000⟩, |1⟩→|111⟩; syndrome identifies one X error.Does not protect against phase errors; majority vote is only a classical analogy.

Quantum error correction never copies an unknown state. It encodes logical information into an entangled subspace and measures syndromes—information about the error, not the protected amplitudes.

08 Mixed States, Density Matrices, and Partial Trace

A density matrix represents either a pure state ρ=|ψ⟩⟨ψ| or an ensemble ρ=Σᵢpᵢ|ψᵢ⟩⟨ψᵢ|. It satisfies ρ†=ρ, Tr(ρ)=1, and ρ≥0. A pure state has Tr(ρ²)=1; a mixed state has Tr(ρ²)<1.

One half of a Bell pair\[\rho_{AB}=|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|\]\[\rho_A=\operatorname{Tr}_B(\rho_{AB})=\tfrac12|0\rangle\langle0|+\tfrac12|1\rangle\langle1|=\frac I2\]The joint state is pure, yet either qubit alone is maximally mixed—an unmistakable signature of entanglement.

09 Advanced Roadmap

QFT & QPE

QFT maps amplitudes into phase-frequency structure. Quantum phase estimation extracts an eigenphase and underpins Shor and many simulation algorithms.

VQE & QAOA

Hybrid variational loops tune parameterized circuits using classical optimization; useful conceptually, but performance depends strongly on noise and trainability.

Quantum ML

Uses quantum feature maps or variational models. Data loading and fair classical comparisons are central; broad practical advantage remains unproven.

Fault tolerance

Logical gates, code distance, thresholds, magic-state distillation, and resource estimates connect abstract algorithms to reliable machines.
